How Solar Panels Work

Solar panels consist of individual solar cells generally crafted from semiconductor materials, predominantly silicon. When sunlight reaches these cells, it carries photons, which are essentially particles of sunlight.

The photovoltaic effect occurs when sunlight interacts with the semiconductor material. Upon contact with a solar cell's surface, a photon can energize an electron, enabling it to break free from its usual position within the material's atomic structure.

The flow of escaped electrons through the semiconductor material generates an electric current, thus producing electricity. Metal conductive plates are strategically positioned on the sides of the solar cell to direct the electrons and stimulate current formation.

For the formation of a solar panel, individual solar cells are interconnected in a series or parallel manner. The collective output of these cells yields a heightened voltage and power output. These solar panels are then aggregated to form a solar array.

The direct current (DC) electricity created by the solar panels is converted into alternating current (AC) by passing through an inverter. AC is the type of electricity utilized in residential and commercial settings.

After the conversion, the AC electricity is integrated into the building's electrical system. It can fuel appliances, lighting, and various electrical devices. Any surplus electricity can either be fed back into the grid or stored in batteries for future utilization.

To enhance efficiency, many solar panel setups incorporate monitoring and control systems. These systems track electricity generation, monitor system health, and ensure optimal utilization of the generated power.
